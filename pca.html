Overview

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a dimensionality reduction technique that simplifies complex datasets while preserving most of the meaningful variance. It is used in this project to identify key nutritional patterns and reduce redundancy among food attributes.

Data Preprocessing

Before applying PCA, the dataset was cleaned and standardized to ensure optimal performance. This involved handling missing values, ensuring all features were numerical, and using Sklearn’s StandardScaler to normalize values.

View Cleaned Data

PCA Preprocessing

The dataset was further processed specifically for PCA. Standardization was applied to ensure a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one for all features, making PCA more effective.

View Preprocessing Script

PCA Variance Retention

To determine the number of components required to retain most of the variance, cumulative variance analysis was performed. The plot below shows the number of principal components needed to retain 95% of the total variance.

The results indicate that five principal components are required to retain at least 95% of the variance. This reduces the complexity of the dataset while maintaining essential information.

Eigenvalues of the Principal Components

Eigenvalues represent the amount of variance captured by each principal component. The top three eigenvalues, which correspond to the three most significant components, are displayed below.

These values provide insight into which components hold the most variance and contribute the most to distinguishing patterns in the dataset.

PCA Visualizations

The following plots illustrate how the data is represented after PCA transformation in both two-dimensional and three-dimensional space.

The 2D projection retains 64.78% of the variance, allowing for basic clustering and pattern recognition based on macronutrient composition.

The 3D PCA projection retains 80.71% of the variance, providing a more detailed visualization of how different food products relate to one another based on their attributes.

Key Findings

Based on the cumulative variance analysis, at least five components are required to retain 95% of the total variance in the dataset. This ensures that the majority of important patterns remain intact while reducing dimensionality.
